Dictionary of Tags & Genres

Adventure

How to tell: If your characters are constantly on the move, facing danger, and never get a break, you’ve got adventure. Plot = journey.

Coming-of-Age

How to tell: If your character starts young or immature and grows into who they’re meant to be by the end, that’s coming-of-age. Angst is required.

Comedy

How to tell: If your main goal is to make the reader laugh—even if bad stuff happens—it’s comedy. Jokes per page should be high.

Crime

How to tell: If criminals, law enforcement, or the justice system are front and center, it’s crime fiction. Think heists, detectives, mafias.

Cyberpunk

How to tell: If your world is high-tech, full of hackers, dystopian corporations, neon lights, and social decay, welcome to cyberpunk.

Dark Fantasy

How to tell: If it’s fantasy but grim, violent, morally twisted, or disturbing—it’s dark fantasy. Think "Game of Thrones" but darker.

Drama

How to tell: If the plot is mostly emotional or interpersonal conflict with little external action, it’s drama. Expect crying or brooding.

Dystopian

How to tell: If society has collapsed or gone full-totalitarian and your characters are stuck surviving or rebelling—it’s dystopian.

Fairy Tale / Mythic

How to tell: If your story has moral lessons, archetypes, talking animals, or magical justice, and feels timeless—it’s mythic or fairy tale-inspired.

Fantasy

How to tell: If your book has magic, made-up creatures, kingdoms, or anything that doesn’t exist in our world—fantasy. Bonus points if there’s a chosen one.

Gothic

How to tell: If it’s creepy, old, dramatic, and drenched in mood—especially with haunted mansions or decaying aristocrats—it’s gothic.

Historical Fiction

How to tell: If your story is set in a real historical time/place and tries to feel accurate to that era (with made-up people), it’s historical fiction.

Horror

How to tell: If your book is meant to scare, disturb, or creep people out, congrats—it’s horror. Doesn’t need gore, but it helps.

Magical Realism

How to tell: If your world is normal but weird magical stuff happens and no one acts like it’s weird—that’s magical realism.

Mystery

How to tell: If someone dies, disappears, or something shady happens and the whole book is about solving it—it’s mystery. Big clue trails, red herrings, the works.

Noir

How to tell: If your world is grimy, cynical, and full of morally grey characters, and the tone is dark and gritty—you’re writing noir. Bonus if it rains a lot.

Paranormal

How to tell: If ghosts, psychics, or unexplained supernatural junk are central, but it’s not full-on fantasy—it’s paranormal.

Post-Apocalyptic

How to tell: If the world already ended and people are living in the ruins, scavenging, or dying from disease, you’re post-apocalyptic.

Psychological

How to tell: If the mind is the battlefield—unreliable narrators, mental breakdowns, and emotional manipulation—it’s psychological fiction.

Satire

How to tell: If your book is mocking society, politics, or culture while pretending to be serious—it’s satire. Sharp wit required.

Science Fiction (Sci-Fi)

How to tell: If there’s futuristic tech, space travel, AI, or science-based “what if?” concepts, you're in sci-fi territory. If your world obeys fake physics, it's sci-fi.

Slice of Life

How to tell: If literally nothing major happens but you focus on the beauty, pain, or monotony of everyday life—slice of life. Vibes over plot.

Steampunk

How to tell: If your world is retro-futuristic with gears, steam-powered tech, airships, and Victorian aesthetics, it’s steampunk.

Surrealism

How to tell: If your book reads like a fever dream or makes no logical sense on purpose, and you're blending reality and fantasy with symbolism—surrealism.

Thriller

How to tell: If the book moves fast and the reader’s sweating with tension, it's a thriller. Stakes are high, often life-or-death, and plot drives everything.

Tragedy

How to tell: If everything goes to hell by the end, and someone important dies or loses it all, you’re writing a tragedy. Happy endings not allowed.

Urban Fiction

How to tell: If it’s set in a modern inner-city with gritty realism, slang-heavy dialogue, and raw themes—urban fiction.

Western

How to tell: If it’s set in the American frontier with cowboys, outlaws, and saloons, you’re writing a western. Gunslingers or bust.

Concept / Structure Tags

Epistolary

How to tell: Told through letters, emails, journal entries, or texts. You're spying on the characters.

Experimental Format

How to tell: The structure breaks rules: poetry chunks, mixed media, footnotes, etc.

Framed Narrative

How to tell: A story within a story. Somebody’s telling the tale from the outside.

Multiple POVs

How to tell: More than one character gets the spotlight. Big for ensemble casts or dual narratives.

Nonlinear Timeline

How to tell: The story jumps around in time. Flashbacks, flash-forwards, scrambled scenes.

Open Ending

How to tell: No real resolution. Leaves readers wondering, arguing, or crying.

Plot Twist Central

How to tell: The plot flips at least once hard enough to break necks. Think “WHAT JUST HAPPENED” moments.

Stream of Consciousness

How to tell: The prose is raw thought. No filters, no pause, just brain vomit (in a good way).

Unreliable Narrator

How to tell: The person telling the story is full of it (lying, delusional, or just wrong).

Vignette-Style

How to tell: Short, snapshot scenes loosely strung together. Less plot, more mood.

World / Setting / Society Tags

Alternate History

How to tell: History took a turn and now things are different but familiar.

Closed Society

How to tell: The world has strict rules, hierarchy, or surveillance. Not always dystopia, but always controlling.

Cultural Deep-Dive

How to tell: Explores a specific culture’s traditions, language, or worldview deeply. Not just a setting—it's the soul.

Hyper-Realism

How to tell: No fantasy, no tech, no flair. Real-life drama cranked to 11.

Isolated Setting

How to tell: Characters are stuck somewhere (island, cabin, ship, apocalypse). No escape, no outside world.

Symbolic World

How to tell: The world reflects the theme (e.g., decaying house = mental breakdown). Heavy on metaphor.

Timeless Setting

How to tell: The story could happen at any time. No phones, no references. Feels mythic or suspended in reality.

Utopian on the Surface

How to tell: Everything looks perfect… until it isn’t. The cracks are the plot.

Character-Centric Tags

Antihero Arc

How to tell: Starts selfish, stays messy, maybe gets better. Maybe not.

Burnout Hero

How to tell: The protagonist is tired, done, and dragging themselves forward anyway. Great for gritty stories.

Charismatic Villain

How to tell: The villain steals every scene. Might outshine the protagonist. Might be the protagonist.

Internal Conflict Heavy

How to tell: Most of the story’s tension comes from inside the character. Less gunfights, more existential crises.

Morally Grey Protagonist

How to tell: Your main character is not a good person. Readers might love or hate them, or both.

Naive Outsider

How to tell: The main character is new to the world/setting and doesn’t understand the rules. Forces exposition naturally.

Obsessed Character

How to tell: A character’s fixated on something or someone. Plot revolves around their obsession.

Power Struggle

How to tell: Constant battle for control in relationships, politics, or survival.

Tone / Mood Tags

Bittersweet

How to tell: Happy and sad at once. Endings hit you in the soul.

Bleak and Brutal

How to tell: No sugarcoating. World sucks, people suck, but you're still reading.

Claustrophobic Atmosphere

How to tell: Everything feels tight, tense, or inescapable. Not always horror, but often unnerving.

Dreamlike

How to tell: Surreal, floaty, and possibly confusing. Like wandering through a foggy memory.

Hopepunk

How to tell: Characters fight for good because it’s hard. Emotionally honest, idealistic, raw.

Lyrical Prose

How to tell: The writing reads like poetry. Not about what happens—about how it's told.

Melancholic Vibe

How to tell: A soft sadness throughout. Not full tragedy, just emotionally heavy.

Tense Silence

How to tell: Big moments told with restraint. The weight of what isn’t said matters more.

Theme-Driven Tags

Belonging vs Isolation

How to tell: Characters either trying to fit in or break free from what defines them.

Cycle of Violence

How to tell: The story explores how violence repeats—through families, systems, or vengeance.

Grief as a Character

How to tell: Loss shapes everything. It haunts the plot, drives choices, and doesn’t go away.

Identity Crisis

How to tell: The main theme is about discovering who the hell you are, or being torn between selves.

Memory and Truth

How to tell: What actually happened? What do we remember happened? Reality’s bending.

Power and Corruption

How to tell: A rise to power that slowly rots the person from the inside.

Survival at Any Cost

How to tell: The story is about what you’ll sacrifice to keep living—morals included.

Other Weird-But-Useful Tags

Character Study

How to tell: Deep dive into one person’s psyche. Not about what happens—about them.

Concept-Driven

How to tell: The whole book exists to explore one “what if?” idea. Plot’s just along for the ride.

Dialogue-Heavy

How to tell: People talk a lot. The tension is in the words, not the action.

Literary Vibes

How to tell: Character-driven, rich writing, heavy themes. The “I’m serious fiction” flex.

Myth-Inspired

How to tell: The story echoes ancient myths, archetypes, or legendary tales in a new skin.

Philosophical

How to tell: The book exists to ask big questions. Answers optional.

Quiet Book

How to tell: Low on plot, high on reflection. Calm, introspective, slow-paced.

Social Commentary

How to tell: The story has things to say about real-world issues, and it's not being subtle.
